Manchester United's 2007-08 season was one of the greatest in the club's history. Under Sir Alex Ferguson, the side completed the European Double, winning both the Premier League and the UEFA Champions League. In the league, United finished with 87 points — 27 wins, 6 draws, and 5 defeats — scoring 80 goals and conceding just 22. The title race went to the final day, with Chelsea finishing two points behind on 85 and Arsenal on 83. Cristiano Ronaldo was the undeniable star, scoring 31 league goals to win the Golden Boot and setting a new benchmark for attacking output from a wide position. Carlos Tevez and Wayne Rooney provided further firepower, while Rio Ferdinand and Nemanja Vidić formed one of the most formidable centre-back pairings in Premier League history.

In Europe, the journey was equally compelling. United topped their Champions League group with 16 points from six matches, then overcame Lyon, Roma, and Barcelona to reach the final in Moscow. There, they faced Chelsea in the first all-English Champions League final. After a 1-1 draw through extra time — Ronaldo's header cancelled out by Frank Lampard — the match went to penalties. John Terry's infamous slip allowed United back into the shootout, and Edwin van der Sar saved Nicolas Anelka's effort to clinch the club's third European Cup. Domestically, the cup runs were less successful: United fell to Coventry City in the League Cup third round and lost to eventual winners Portsmouth in the FA Cup sixth round. Nevertheless, the league and Champions League double cemented the 2007-08 squad as one of the finest in Manchester United's history, blending experienced veterans like Ryan Giggs and Paul Scholes with emerging talents like Anderson and Nani, and world-class performers like Ronaldo, Rooney, and Tevez at the peak of their powers.

03 / TACTICS

Tactics

Sir Alex Ferguson's Manchester United operated in a flexible 4-4-2 system that could shift into a 4-4-1-1 or 4-2-3-1 depending on the opponent and the phase of the game. The foundation was the Ferdinand–Vidić partnership at centre-back, two defenders with contrasting but complementary qualities: Ferdinand's composure and distribution alongside Vidić's aggression and aerial dominance. Behind them, Van der Sar provided the calmness and command of a world-class goalkeeper, while Evra and Brown pushed high from the full-back positions to create width and support attacks.

In midfield, the balance between Scholes and Carrick at the base was crucial. Scholes provided the creative spark and long-range passing, while Carrick controlled tempo and recycled possession. The wide positions were where United generated their most devastating threat. Ronaldo on the right was given enormous freedom to drift inside, shoot from distance, and attack the penalty area, while Giggs, Nani, or Park offered different solutions on the opposite flank — Giggs with intelligence and passing, Nani with directness, Park with pressing energy. Hargreaves could also slot into the centre or at right-back, adding steel and defensive discipline.

The front two of Rooney and Tevez gave the side relentless energy. Both were willing to press from the front, drop deep to link play, and run the channels. Rooney offered power and creativity; Tevez brought intensity and an extraordinary work rate. Saha, when available, provided a different option with his pace and finishing ability. Ferguson rotated his squad skilfully throughout the season, keeping players fresh across all competitions and adapting his system depending on the challenge. Against Barcelona in the Champions League semi-final, for example, the side was set up to defend deeper and counter-attack, while in the league the approach was typically more aggressive and front-footed.

The hallmark of this team was its mentality. Time and again, United scored late goals to rescue or win matches — Tevez at Blackburn, Tevez at Tottenham, Carrick against Manchester City. Ferguson's ability to manage the squad, manage the occasion, and instil a belief that the game was never lost until the final whistle was perhaps the greatest tactical weapon at the club's disposal. The 2007-08 side combined experience, youth, individual brilliance, and collective resolve in a way that produced one of the most successful seasons in the history of English football.
