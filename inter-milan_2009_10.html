Inter Milan's 2009-10 season stands as the greatest in the club's history and one of the most remarkable campaigns in European football. Under the masterful guidance of José Mourinho, the Nerazzurri became the first and only Italian team to win the historic Treble: Serie A, the Coppa Italia, and the UEFA Champions League. Following the departure of Zlatan Ibrahimović, the squad was brilliantly reconstructed. New arrivals Diego Milito, Samuel Eto'o, Wesley Sneijder, Lúcio, and Thiago Motta integrated flawlessly, creating a highly disciplined, tactically astute, and ruthless team.

In Serie A, Inter faced a fierce challenge from Roma but secured the Scudetto on the final day with an 82-point tally, conceding just 34 goals. In Europe, they produced iconic defensive masterclasses and clinical counter-attacks, notably eliminating Chelsea and Barcelona before defeating Bayern Munich 2-0 in the Madrid final. Domestically, they edged out Roma in the Coppa Italia final. The 2009-10 side epitomized Mourinho's philosophy: a united brotherhood capable of adapting to any opponent, defending with absolute resolve, and striking with devastating precision. It remains the gold standard of Italian club football.

03 / TACTICS

Tactics

Tactically, José Mourinho transformed Inter into a formidable 4-2-3-1 machine, moving away from the previous 4-3-1-2 diamond. The defensive foundation was built on an impenetrable center-back pairing of Lúcio and Walter Samuel, flanked by the relentless Maicon on the right and Javier Zanetti or Cristian Chivu on the left. In midfield, Esteban Cambiasso anchored the side, intercepting play and initiating transitions, usually partnered by the physical and composed Thiago Motta.

The true tactical revolution happened in the attacking third. Wesley Sneijder operated as the ultimate number 10, utilizing his elite vision and two-footed distribution to unlock defenses on the counter. The wide roles demanded immense sacrifice; Samuel Eto'o, a natural striker, famously reinvented himself as a hard-working left-winger, while Goran Pandev provided balance on the right. Up front, Diego Milito was the flawless focal point, offering exceptional hold-up play, clever movement, and lethal finishing. Inter was characterized by its low-block resilience, unmatched psychological fortitude, and lightning-fast transitions.
