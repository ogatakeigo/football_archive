Real Madrid's 2014-15 season under Carlo Ancelotti was a rollercoaster of spectacular football and ultimate heartbreak. Following the historic "La Décima" triumph, the squad was bolstered by the arrivals of World Cup stars James Rodríguez, Toni Kroos, and Keylor Navas. The team played breathtaking, attacking football, embarking on a remarkable Spanish-record 22-match winning streak across all competitions during the autumn and winter, capturing both the UEFA Super Cup and the FIFA Club World Cup.

However, injuries to key players like Luka Modrić and James Rodríguez derailed their momentum in the new year. Despite amassing 92 points and scoring a staggering 118 goals in La Liga, they finished two points behind a treble-winning Barcelona. Cristiano Ronaldo had an extraordinary individual campaign, netting 48 league goals to win the Pichichi trophy. In the cup competitions, Madrid was eliminated by Atlético Madrid in the Copa del Rey Round of 16, and their Champions League defense ended in the semi-finals against Juventus. Ultimately, despite the dazzling football and individual brilliance, ending the season without a major domestic or European trophy led to Ancelotti's departure, closing a memorable yet frustrating chapter.

03 / TACTICS

Tactics

Real Madrid under Carlo Ancelotti were characterized by an incredibly fluid and devastatingly potent attacking system, nominally structured as a 4-3-3 but highly adaptable depending on the phase of play. With the departure of Ángel Di María and Xabi Alonso, Ancelotti deployed Toni Kroos as the deep-lying playmaker, flanked by Luka Modrić and James Rodríguez (or Isco). This technically brilliant midfield trio excelled in retaining possession, switching play, and supplying the formidable "BBC" front line.

In attack, Cristiano Ronaldo operated from the left but was given absolute freedom to drift centrally as a primary goalscorer, resulting in his astonishing 48 league goals. Gareth Bale provided natural width and explosive directness on the right, while Karim Benzema served as the ultimate facilitator—dropping deep to link play and create space for the wingers cutting inside. Full-backs Marcelo and Dani Carvajal pushed aggressively high up the pitch, effectively acting as wingers in possession and ensuring the team maintained immense attacking width.

Defensively, the team relied on the athletic dominance and recovery pace of Sergio Ramos, Pepe, and Raphaël Varane. When out of possession, the shape often morphed into a 4-4-2, with Bale or James dropping into the midfield line to provide balance. While this system produced some of the most breathtaking, high-scoring football in Europe and powered a 22-match winning streak, it placed a heavy physical burden on the starting XI. Ultimately, critical injuries to Modrić and James disrupted the team's midfield control and tempo, exposing defensive vulnerabilities that cost them major titles in the crucial final months of the season.
